Detroit Shock Fan Lifecycle & CRM Intelligence Dashboard

Power BI dashboard using fan-level modeling, lifecycle segmentation, data quality checks, and next-best-action strategy.

📌 Project Overview

This project expands my original Detroit Shock fan analytics work into a more advanced CRM intelligence dashboard. The dashboard analyzes fan behavior across engagement, ticketing, and merchandise activity to identify lifecycle stages, behavioral segments, fan value, retention opportunities, and recommended marketing actions.

🎯 Business Challenge

A sports organization needs more than basic fan reporting. Leadership needs to understand which fans are loyal, which fans are slipping away, which audiences have conversion potential, and what actions should be taken to improve retention, revenue, and engagement.

The goal was to transform raw fan, engagement, ticketing, and merchandise data into a structured Power BI dashboard that supports CRM strategy and lifecycle marketing decisions.

🛠️ Process

  • Imported fan, engagement, ticket purchase, and merchandise datasets into Power BI.
  • Built relationships using fan_id to create a clean fan-level data model.
  • Created a unified Fan_Master table to combine demographic, engagement, ticketing, and merchandise behavior.
  • Developed DAX logic for fan value, engagement score, recency, lifecycle stage, fan segment, and next-best-action recommendations.
  • Added data quality flags to identify records with transaction activity but missing purchase dates.
  • Designed a three-page Power BI dashboard focused on executive summary, fan exploration, and CRM strategy.

📊 Dashboard Page 1 — Executive Overview

The Executive Overview page summarizes the overall fan base, including total fans, average fan value, average engagement, percentage of at-risk fans, lifecycle distribution, and fan segment distribution.

Executive Overview — lifecycle, segmentation, and key fan metrics.

Key Takeaways

  • The majority of fans fall into the casual segment, representing a large conversion opportunity.
  • At-risk fans make up a meaningful portion of the fan base and should be prioritized for retention campaigns.
  • VIP and loyal fans represent high-value audiences that should be protected through exclusive offers and personalized engagement.

🔍 Dashboard Page 2 — Fan Deep Dive

The Fan Deep Dive page allows users to filter fans by lifecycle stage, segment, gender, and age while viewing fan-level details such as total fan value, engagement score, days since last activity, and recommended next best action.

Fan Deep Dive — interactive fan-level exploration and filtering.

Key Takeaways

  • Stakeholders can quickly isolate high-value fans, at-risk fans, and specific behavioral segments.
  • The fan-level table connects customer attributes directly to recommended actions.
  • This page supports CRM targeting, campaign planning, and audience prioritization.

🎯 Dashboard Page 3 — Strategy & Next Best Action

The Strategy & Next Best Action page translates fan behavior into business recommendations. It highlights where marketing efforts should focus across retention, conversion, upsell, and VIP engagement strategies.

Strategy & Next Best Action — CRM recommendations by lifecycle and segment.

Key Takeaways

  • Most recommended actions focus on introductory offers, reflecting a large casual fan base with conversion potential.
  • At-risk high-value fans are a small but important audience for VIP retention efforts.
  • Digital fans can be converted through app and email-based ticket or merchandise promotions.
  • VIP and loyal fans should receive exclusive perks, early access, and premium engagement opportunities.

🧪 Data Quality & Assumptions

Issues Identified

  • Missing transaction dates: Some fans had ticket or merchandise spend but missing last purchase dates. These records were flagged using Missing_Ticket_Date_Flag and Missing_Merch_Date_Flag.
  • Channel ambiguity: Some fans had merchandise spend but no team shop visits, suggesting online purchases or differences in how store visits were tracked.
  • No recorded activity: Fans with no email, ticket, or merchandise activity were assigned Days_Since_Last_Activity = -1 and treated as dormant.
  • Historical activity skew: Some fans had more than 1,000 days since last activity. Rather than modifying dates, lifecycle thresholds were adjusted to reflect the historical nature of the data.

Modeling Assumptions

  • Lifecycle stages were based on observed recency, engagement, and value patterns.
  • The “New” lifecycle stage was excluded because the dataset did not include acquisition or join date fields.
  • Next-best-action recommendations were rule-based and designed to demonstrate CRM decisioning logic.

💡 Business Recommendations

  • Launch introductory ticket or merchandise offers to convert casual fans into higher-value segments.
  • Prioritize at-risk fans with re-engagement campaigns before they become fully dormant.
  • Use app and email campaigns to convert digital fans into purchasers.
  • Protect VIP and loyal fans through exclusive perks, early access, and premium experiences.
  • Continue monitoring data quality issues that impact recency and lifecycle classification.
